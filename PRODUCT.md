# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Recruiters, hiring managers, and engineering leads evaluating Lewis Sawe for cloud engineering and DevOps roles. They arrive from LinkedIn, GitHub, or a shared link. They scan fast: credentials first, proof of work second, personality third. Secondary audience: fellow AWS community builders and cloud engineers who find the site through blog posts, YouTube videos, or conference mentions.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio that proves Lewis Sawe builds real infrastructure, not just lists certifications. The site should make a hiring manager think "this person ships" within 10 seconds. Ten AWS and cloud certifications, hands-on projects, published content, and a working serverless contact form back that claim. Success: the visitor clicks through to a project, watches a video, or sends a message.

## Brand Personality

Technical, confident, approachable. Lewis is the engineer who can explain VPC peering to a non-technical PM and then go build it. The voice is direct without being cold, knowledgeable without being academic, and casual enough to crack a joke about 3 AM incident pages. Not a hacker cosplay; a working engineer who happens to enjoy the craft.

Three-word personality: **sharp, grounded, builder**.

## Anti-references

- **Generic developer portfolios**: dark mode with cyan/neon accents, particle backgrounds, glitch text, "matrix rain" loading screens, terminal-themed everything. The current site has these patterns and they read as template, not personal.
- **Certification walls**: badge grids with no context. The certs matter, but they need to serve a narrative, not fill a section.
- **SaaS landing page structure**: hero metrics, identical feature cards, "boost your productivity" energy. This is a person, not a product.
- **Overproduced agency sites**: parallax everything, scroll-jacking, animations that delay content. Recruiters are busy; don't make them wait.
- **Hacker/cyberpunk cosplay**: the "I am a l33t hacker" aesthetic. Lewis is a professional cloud engineer, not a movie character.

## Design Principles

1. **Proof over claims.** Every section should demonstrate capability, not just state it. A working Lambda contact form says more than a bullet point about serverless experience.
2. **Respect the visitor's time.** Recruiters scan in seconds. Lead with the strongest signal, make navigation instant, never block content behind animations or loading screens.
3. **Personal, not performative.** The site should feel like Lewis built it because he wanted to, not because a template told him to. Personality comes from voice and content choices, not visual effects.
4. **Substance carries the design.** Ten certifications, real projects, published videos and articles. Let the depth of work create the impression. The design supports that depth; it doesn't compete with it.
5. **One site, one voice.** Every section should feel like the same person wrote it. Consistent tone from the hero to the contact form.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Baseline: WCAG 2.1 AA on all pages. Key commitments:
- Color contrast ratios verified, not eyeballed. Current cyan-on-dark combinations need checking.
- All interactive elements keyboard-navigable with visible focus states.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` respected for all animations (glitch effects, particle background, typing animations, scroll reveals).
- Semantic HTML first. The terminal contact form needs accessible labeling for screen readers.
- Loading screen must not trap focus or block access for assistive technology users.
